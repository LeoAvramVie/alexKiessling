Statements & Essays Statements & Essays

Alex Kiessling (Artist)

Der Traum des Individuums

Embedded in the ‘anchored’ hinges of reality lies the individual’s dream, which carries the desire for immortality deep within itself. In my work, I deal with the mind’s demand for eternity and the collapse of the organic, the behaviors associated with this collapse, and the possible alternatives within existence. At the same time, the intersections and contact levels depict the focus of my interest: the existence between reality and dream; between the truth, which is able to be experienced individually, and the irrational.

In the static scenes of my paintings, the protagonists remain mostly resident between the glaring colorfulness of virtual realities and darkness, which is inherent in most of our dream sequences and memories. Both of these worlds are paramount due to their systematic character, which is connected to the simulative, and are projection surfaces of the human psyche.

Wishful thinking and nightmarish fears collide with equal power into each other and, directly through their contrasts, clarify the tragedy of man.

Alex Kiessling (Artist)

Vielschichtigkeit menschlicher Daseinsebenen

In short, I would define the foundation of my artistic work as being the examination of the complexity of man’s levels of existence. How do positions within a society come about, how do we define our identity, where does individuality begin and where does it end, how and why are status symbols used in the everyday global consumer culture, how is the interaction between the subconscious and the conscious both as an individual and in a crowd, and to what degree do dreams bridge the communication gap between the aforementioned poles of our existence? Regarding the question about dreams, I differentiate between 3 various, yet strongly related orientations: firstly, personal dream sequences during sleep, then medial sequences which can trigger dreams or induce “hopes (goals)”, and finally systems which present dream-like structures, such as video games and virtual realities, which function as “alternate worlds”. These 3 categories most clearly reflect mans’ desires, fears, and drives, and they provide the subconscious motives and matters of our lives with a meaningful manifestation and simultaneously with a language that, through its structured sounds, makes these matters able to be recognized and interpreted.

Günther Oberhollenzer (Kunsthistoriker)

Zeit komprimiert im Medium der Malerei

Our daily perception is increasingly determined by a flood of visual material; we are constantly exposed to countless digital images. Painting can, I believe, ensure a kind of deceleration; it enables – whether as an artist painting or as a viewer – a pause in the midst of this flood of images. This is something I like to say about painting.
However, Alex Kiessling’s new works put me in a bit of an embarrassment. Because when I stand in front of his paintings, this thought begins to wobble. Kiessling’s portraits vehemently refuse a pause, a focus, a concentration of the gaze. Quite the opposite. They make the gaze restless – searching for a reference point, a vanishing point, a quiet place, we scan the faces without finding a resting pole. But it is not only the eye that is in motion: the paintings challenge viewing from different perspectives. We walk the picture, dance around, towards the picture, then away again, look at it obliquely from the sides… Yes, I believe the paintings come alive precisely through the varied distances and viewing positions.
"Statics and dynamics," says Kiessling, determine his work again and again. We know the "Head spins" of 2010 – heads depicted from different perspectives next to or behind one another – from the front, from the side – heads that move back and forth but nevertheless remain static movement sequences (as contradictory as that may sound), conserved, captured, eternalized by painting.
In the new portraits of human heads on display here, Kiessling seems to want to break open precisely this problem: by splitting and pulling apart several layers of color or glaze that usually lie on top of one another, a tense dynamic arises, an optical noise that leaves the viewer unclear whether the motif is currently assembling or dissolving. The pictures seem to represent a moment that includes a before and after. Time compressed in the medium of painting.
And it is a movement that becomes independent. Yet it is not the person or the head that moves, but rather its depiction. The painted image is blurry, our view of it disrupted. The pictures flicker, are sharp and unsharp at the same time. You cannot concentrate your gaze, hold the picture, it tilts away again and again, slips away.
The portraits – painted in fine layers of glaze, reduced, in front of a white background – are less plastic than earlier works, they are more transparent, also more delicate. The former physicality is stronger withdrawn, the two-dimensionality becomes more important. The split, overlapping colors remind of the color impression obtained when "white light" is split into its spectral colors by a prism. In their technical execution, they also recall film stills / film frames that shift, that lie on top of / next to one another.
Kiessling is, as he says himself, a "perfectionist artist," everything must be painted very precisely, "I like the shadows where they belong." He loves the precise, the exact; but he tries to avoid anything obsessive. It is a struggle between the realistic, the not wanting to leave the figure, and allowing the painterly, the accidental.
His paintings fascinate because of their technical precision, but in the new works he also repeatedly allows – and I like this very much – the slightly unfinished, the unperfect. Traces of the painting process, streaks and fine running traces are visible, brushstrokes like overpaintings recognizable. This succeeded particularly well in the watercolors: details blur, body shapes become imprecise, "the water works and paints along."
The heads were always preliminary studies for larger, narrative works. But this time Kiessling detaches himself from the study character; the heads are the main theme of the exhibition and consistently, stringently implemented. The artist asks fundamental questions: How do we perceive our existence? Or: Where are we moving? It is not a purely physical appearance of the depicted that has meaning, but rather an emotional presence, detached from the body. A feeling, a moment, perhaps also an intangible memory…
It seems almost as if the artist wants to transfer or capture the hectic nature of our time in the portrait-like images, representations that nevertheless remain diffuse. His paintings are thus also symbols for the insecure present, which we can no longer fix, hold, precisely define.
Speaking of time: the "timeliness" and "modernity" of painting is repeatedly questioned, or at least it is attempted to convict this classical genre of fine art of being outdated and antiquated compared to the new media. Controversial discussions about the meaning and future of painting have also been led in recent times. Actually an old hat, because in the last hundred, indeed two hundred years, painting was declared dead several times and it was repeatedly claimed that it no longer has a place in the present.
With the rise of photography in the 19th century, it was believed painting would disappear sooner or later. But the opposite was the case. Through the invention of photography, it was able to free itself from the "ballast" of the depiction. The interest shifted from the motif to the painting style, the picture became a two-dimensional field in which an order of shapes and colors is relevant – a parallel reality to the world. And so, in our time too, painting can seek its own paths of expression in dialogue with the present, let itself be inspired by the new media or refer to its very own strengths.
Back to Kiessling. He creates paintings that are very much rooted in today, of which I cannot imagine anyone painting like this 50 years ago. He reacts in the classic medium of the canvas painting to uncertainties and questions of our present, to a multimedia society in which we are bombarded daily with thousands of images that make it difficult for us to concentrate, to sharpen our gaze, he reacts to a virtual world, full of photos, movies and information, in which we hardly get an overview…
And I believe precisely today, in such a digitized world, the need of people for painted pictures, for the analog, tangible original, is growing again, the demand and longing for authenticity and the personality of the painting artist is rising. Just like those of Alex Kiessling.

Alex Kiessling (Artist)

Fragmentierte Identität

In a fast growing bubble of dreams and reality the urban space explodes in all directions. The environment for the existence of life is changing rapidly into something different. This process of transformation massively affects the behavior and the habits of human beings. Especially the psyche of the individual has to cope and prevail over increasing requirements of its surrounding. A new term that appears in the dawn of the 21st century frequently is “fragmented identity”. It describes a state of existence in which we have to combine different and highly developed parts of our identity at the same time and switch between them permanently. There is not one “world” anymore, but there are dozens of parallel-worlds and most of them are virtual.

In my work I concentrate on dreams and all kinds of dreamlike structures and explore its borders and bridges to reality. I try to visualize the “no men`s land” between the absurdity in our existence and the concrete concerns that come with our human mind or spirit. I am fascinated by the interacting vibrations between virtual reality, dreams and the basic common ground of our world`s so called reality.

Alex Kiessling (Artist)

Zoon Politicon

Aristotle describes man as a Zoon Politikon. His observation is directed at the goal-oriented behavior of man, which in its essence strives for a more complex order. In Aristotle, this is specifically the development of human potential in and through the founding of the city/state – the polis.

If one follows this teleological thought, it does not necessarily end with the Aristotelian polis, but can be extended further – depending on one's imagination. The more complex the structures of coexistence become, the more fragmented and individual the roles or tasks of the individual being become. And all the more diverse the vectors of the courses of action that cause the complex, communicative coexistence to escalate. Heights and faults of human existence not only increase quantitatively, but their extreme manifestations also move closer together. Another thought of the exhibition is that, strictly speaking, the polis cannot exist without animal existence itself. Only through cooperation, subjugation, and exploitation of other living beings did man manage to reach today's level of existence. "Zoon Politicon" is an attempt to isolate a tiny fraction of the fragments of our actions from their sequential logic, to work them out in new image structures, and to clarify them – to illuminate the play of social and societal forces, and to critically question traditional behavior patterns. The exhibition title is also to be understood in multiple ways, as the animal – Zoon – is deliberately placed in the foreground of the figurative visual language. The animals thus function as placeholders and lend an indefinite drama to the respective image situations in their role-play. The subjects are an attempt to translate popular cultural image motifs and codings into the language of contemporary, figurative painting.

Christian Bazant-Hegemark (Artist)

Volatile Portraits

The exhibition focuses a series of large scale portraits circling volatile aspects of interpersonal encounters. Using shift effects that get in the way of seeing the portrayed people, Alex Kiessling breaks open the classical portrait tradition, subverting its original purpose. The tradition thus changes into a medium used to discuss contemporary digital imaging, connecting aspects of rotoscoping and data moshing.
The works offer a high degree of perfection, while questioning the art of portraiture. It also makes obvious the artist’s interest in digital image manipulation – instead of celebrating the portrait’s illusion of permanence, the work’s central theme seems the volatility of contemporary depicting systems.
With the new series, Kiessling departs from his previous series of hyper real portraits, which focused traumatic aspects of everyday settings. The new work seems stronger, stricter, more adamant – having disposed of the previous work’s backgrounds, it’s now solely the remaining people that operate within the canvases. At the same time, the shifts prevent any kind of visual clarity – the depicted people remain invisible, the visual effect surrounding them being the only thing to witness.

Alex Kiessling (Artist)

Long Distance Art

This is a Project that happened on September 26th 2013 where i was working simultaneously with two industrial robots. One was situated in London (Trafalgar Square), the other one in Berlin (Breitscheidplatz/Gedächtniskirche), while i was working in Vienna (Museumsquartier/Ovalhalle). Together the maschines and i created several triptychs, where each drawing works as a counterpart to the two other pieces. It was and still is is an exciting project with a lot of topics to be discussed.
